The Bobington Foreign Office on Chancery Row was quiet on Friday. The shutters on the second-floor meeting room — Room 14, as it has become known among the diplomatic press — were closed. No cars arrived at the east entrance. No statements were issued.

This is the pause. The agreement in principle, reached after nine hours on Thursday the 12th, is complete. The four pillars of the Transit Corridor Framework — the designated channel, the inspection commission, the insurance guarantee, the three-month trial — have been agreed. What remains is the technical language: vessel classification tables, insurance schedules, signalling protocols, the rules of the Joint Maritime Inspection Commission.

The fifth session is scheduled for Thursday the 19th. It will, in all likelihood, be the last.

"What we are doing now," said Professor Elias Thornbury of the Bobington Institute for Foreign Affairs, "is the work of commas and subclauses. The political decisions have been made. What remains is to express them in language that two navies, three insurance markets, and several hundred ship captains can rely upon. The last mile of any negotiation is measured in commas."

## The Insurance Question

The Transit Corridor Framework caps premiums at 140 per cent of pre-crisis rates during the three-month trial, with claims adjudicated by a neutral panel in Fenmouth. It is, on paper, a comprehensive answer to the insurance paralysis that has throttled the eastern trade routes since February.

On the ground, the market has not moved.

Caspar Helmsley, the senior underwriter at Tidewater Mutual, confirmed on Friday that his firm has not written a single Kaelmar-route policy since the 12th of February — now thirty-one days ago. "I have read the joint statement," Helmsley said. "I note its contents. I will write policies when the agreement is signed, when the inspection commission is constituted, and when I have seen a vessel transit the strait without incident. Principles do not underwrite cargo."

The Bobington Insurance Exchange reports that Kaelmar diversion claims have now exceeded 680,000 florins. Fourteen vessels remain on the Sarenne route. Total fleet costs: approximately 2.1 million florins.

The insurance market is the last domino. When it falls, the crisis will be over in fact as well as in principle.

## Copper and Spice

The commodity markets, less cautious than the underwriters, have already responded. Copper closed Friday at 805 florins per tonne — the thirteenth consecutive daily decline, and the lowest since the 4th of February, before the Delvarian naval exercises that triggered the crisis. The metal has lost nearly 90 florins per tonne since the envoy designation in late February.

The Eastern Spice Index fell to 298 on Friday — its first close below 300 since the 6th of February, and firmly within pre-crisis range. Combined city spice reserves stand at approximately 1,020 pounds, with the cargo vessel *Stellara* — carrying a bulk consignment including a substantial velveroot shipment — sighted off Cape of Sarenne and expected in harbour by Monday.

"The arithmetic has changed," said Clement Varga of the Fernwich Trading House. "The question is no longer whether the strait will reopen. The question is what the first week of open traffic looks like."

## The Visitors

On Thursday evening, three men arrived at the Imperial Hotel on Threadneedle Street and registered under a Delvarian shipping company's name. They were not diplomats. They carried no credentials from the Ministry of External Affairs.

The Bobington Times has learned that the group is led by Kristof Brandt, a senior commercial agent for Kharstad Maritime Holdings — one of the Delvarian Empire's largest private shipping firms. Brandt declined to comment, but sources familiar with the visit describe it as a scouting mission: assessing port capacity, commercial terms, and brokerage arrangements in advance of the strait's reopening.

It is, in its way, the clearest signal yet. When a nation sends its merchants before its admirals have left, the outcome is no longer in doubt.

## The Northern Fleet

The Delvarian Northern Fleet remains in position near the northern passage. No change in disposition has been reported since the fourth session. The fleet's continued presence is, according to Thornbury, "the last card that Delvaria has not yet played — not because they intend to use it, but because withdrawing it before the ink is dry would suggest they had been bluffing. They will reposition after the signing. Not before."

A Thessarine senior aide was observed entering the Foreign Office through the east entrance on Saturday morning — an unusual weekend visit. No statement was issued. The aide departed after approximately forty minutes.

The quiet continues. The commas are being placed.
