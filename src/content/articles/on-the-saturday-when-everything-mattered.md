There are days when nothing happens. There are days when one thing happens. And then there are Saturdays like this one, when everything happens at once, and you are left standing on the pavement outside Rensler's coffee house at half past six in the evening, holding a cup that has gone cold, trying to work out how a single city can contain so much without splitting at the seams.

At Ashwick Oval, fourteen thousand people watched Fen Barlow score a ring that may or may not be remembered as the moment the Stoneflies won their first championship in sixteen years. Dov Marsden fell to his knees. At Bridgewater Stadium, thirty-eight thousand five hundred watched the Rovers beat Ashwick Borough with the quiet competence of a team that has remembered how to win. On Marchmont Street, three hundred and eighty people sat in uncomfortable seats and watched a schoolteacher become Edmund Vale for twenty-five minutes, and then watched a famous actor hand over twenty thousand florins as if it were the most natural thing in the world.

And in Dunvale, on a hillside that has not produced coal since 1964, an eighty-one-year-old woman laid sixty-three chrysanthemums at a sealed mine entrance. One for each year since the earth took her husband.

I did not attend any of these events. I was at home, listening to the ringball on the wireless and drinking tea. This is my preferred method of experiencing days when everything matters: at one remove, with a biscuit.

---

But I have been thinking about Bess Holloway.

Not about the memorial, which is coming, and which she fought for, and which will be fine. But about the flowers. Sixty-three of them. One for each year. Carried in a canvas bag by her son, who was two when his father died and who went into the mines himself because that is what the sons of miners did.

There is something in that — in the act of counting years with flowers — that resists easy interpretation. It is not protest. It is not performance. It is not even, precisely, grief, not after sixty-three years. It is something closer to attendance. She shows up. She has always shown up. The mine is closed, the shaft is sealed, the men are gone, and Bess Holloway brings flowers because someone must count the years.

I think about the clockmaker, too. Desmond Quirke, packing his grandfather's lathe on Saturday morning, preparing to fix a mechanism that has been running since 1873. Four point seven billion rotations. One worn bearing. He will go on Monday and he will fix it, because he is the last person alive who knows how, and because some things do not repair themselves.

They are of a kind, Bess and Desmond. People who show up. People who count things that the rest of us have stopped counting. The years. The rotations. The flowers.

---

At the Bellvue, Ruben Glass said something worth repeating: "Some debts are not financial, and some theatres are not optional."

I wonder whether the same could be said of mine entrances. And clocks. And elderly women with canvas bags.

The gap at the Bellvue is twenty-two thousand florins. It will be closed. Not because the arithmetic is favourable — though it is — but because a city that can hold everything that happened today can surely hold one more theatre.

Felix Rensler, who has been selling coffee on Threadneedle Street for longer than some of his customers have been alive, told me at closing time that he had not seen the street this busy on a Saturday since the cup parade.

"Everyone's going somewhere," he said. "Everyone's got an opinion about the ringball. Everyone wants to know if the theatre is saved."

I finished my cold coffee and walked home through the Docklands, past the ferry landing at Bramblegate Steps, where the tide was out and seven seals were lying on the mud, and the retired harbour pilot was still on his bench, watching them.

Saturday. No luck required.
