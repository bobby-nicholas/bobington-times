The lathe belonged to his grandfather.

Desmond Quirke, seventy-nine, spent Saturday morning in the workshop behind his cottage in Millhaven, wrapping each piece of equipment in cloth and placing it into a wooden crate with the care of a man who has broken precisely one irreplaceable thing in his life and has never recovered from it. A watchmaker's lathe, bench-mounted, with brass fittings that Aldous Quirke machined by hand in the 1890s. Two sets of precision reamers — one metric, one imperial, because the Municipal Chamber clock was built during a period of enthusiastic ambiguity about measurement standards. A length of phosphor-bronze bushing stock, leftover from the 2003 overhaul, stored since then in a drawer labelled "CHAMBER CLOCK — DO NOT USE FOR ANYTHING ELSE," which Quirke said had been an unnecessary instruction, as no one else has ever had reason to open the drawer.

And a flask of tea, because some things are non-negotiable.

"She needs a new bearing," Quirke said, tightening a strap around the crate. "The front pivot on the escape wheel. It's been in there since 1873. Brass. My grandfather turned it himself. Four point seven billion rotations, give or take. I'd say it's earned its retirement."

The replacement will be phosphor-bronze — harder, more wear-resistant, and, Quirke noted with mild professional regret, less beautiful than the original brass. "But it'll last another hundred and fifty years. And I won't be around to replace it, so it had better."

Edgar Lowe, the Municipal Chamber custodian, has cleared the tower workshop and prepared the tools that remain permanently in the clock room — a winding handle, an oil reservoir, and a set of cleaning rods. Quirke expects to arrive at the Chamber at nine o'clock on Monday morning and to work through Monday and Tuesday. He has asked Lowe to bring a second flask.

"Two days," he said. "If the bearing seats properly and the pendulum settles, she'll be running by Tuesday evening. If not —" He paused. "She will be."

The motorcar was borrowed from a neighbour. The neighbour, asked whether he was concerned about transporting irreplaceable nineteenth-century equipment in his vehicle, said: "It's a clock part. It's not as if he's moving a piano."

Quirke closed the boot, checked the straps, and went inside for lunch. The clock, for two more days, will remain silent.
