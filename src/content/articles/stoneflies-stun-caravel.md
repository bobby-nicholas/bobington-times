They have waited sixteen years. They will have to wait one more week.

But what a week it will be. And what an afternoon this was.

In front of fourteen thousand souls — packed into every terrace, standing bay, and temporary wooden bench along the western embankment of Ashwick Oval — the Ashwick Stoneflies ended the Caravel Harriers' title defence with a 27-25 victory that will rank among the finest ringball matches played in this or any other city.

Fen Barlow scored the decisive ring. Of course he did. The twenty-two-year-old from nowhere, playing his eleventh senior match, received the drop at the edge of the right lodge with four minutes remaining in the final quarter, rolled his marker with a shoulder feint that left the man clutching air, and sent a flat, rising arc through the ring from eighteen yards. The sound that followed was not a cheer. It was a detonation.

Dov Marsden, thirty-six years old, sixteen years without a championship, sank to his knees at the final whistle and stayed there. His teammates piled around him. In the stands, strangers embraced. The Caravel contingent — some eight hundred souls in the away enclosure at the southern end — stood in silence, then, to their considerable credit, applauded.

## The Match

Caravel came to Ashwick as champions and favourites. They had Oska Dain, the finest centrist in the league. They had a three-point lead at the top of the table. They had the quiet, remorseless precision that had delivered back-to-back titles under Wil Sørensen.

The Stoneflies had something else. They had the Oval.

The first quarter was cagey, as expected. Dain probed from the centreline, finding angles that lesser centrists would not have seen. Marsden matched him, sitting deeper than usual, denying space. The only ring came from Petra Venn, who took a sharp pass from Barlow at the left lodge and arced it through the ring with the economical motion of someone posting a letter. Stoneflies 7, Caravel 4 at the first break.

The second quarter belonged to Marsden. At thirty-six, with bad knees and a back that does not straighten easily before noon, Dov Marsden played twenty minutes of ringball that would have graced any era. He controlled the tempo, moved the ball through the lodges with unhurried intelligence, and then — in the fourteenth minute — launched an arc from the centreline that cleared every outstretched hand and passed through the ring so cleanly that the net barely moved.

The Oval erupted. Stoneflies 14, Caravel 9 at the half.

## Caravel Answer

Champions do not go quietly. The third quarter was Dain's. Released by Sørensen into a roaming role, he dismantled the Stoneflies' lodge structure with a series of passes that arrived at their destinations before the defenders knew they had been sent. His first ring came in the fourth minute — a sweeping arc from the right side that curled and dipped. His second came eight minutes later, a sharp, flat strike from close range after Caravel worked the ball through three pairs of hands in four seconds.

At 18-20, Caravel led for the first time. The away enclosure found its voice. The Oval fell quiet — not silent, but holding its breath.

Maren Thatch kept the Stoneflies alive. The keeper, who has been a fortress all season, produced three clearances in the final six minutes of the third quarter, the last a diving interception at the base of the ring post that drew gasps from both ends. Barlow added a ring on the counter — a rising arc from the right lodge that restored the lead. Stoneflies 21, Caravel 20 entering the final quarter.

## The Fourth Quarter

Twenty minutes of ringball. Three points in it. Fourteen thousand people who had forgotten how to sit down.

Dain had a chance in the seventh minute. He received the drop cleanly, beat his marker, shaped to arc — and Thatch read it. The clearance was emphatic. The Oval roared.

Marsden, who had been magnificent all afternoon, found Venn at the left lodge in the eleventh minute. Venn's arc was blocked, but the ball broke loose and Barlow was there. He did not think. He arced.

The ball climbed. It seemed to hang in the March sunlight — pale, watery, the kind of light that makes the Oval glow — and then it fell through the ring, and Fen Barlow raised both arms, and the noise was like the sea.

Stoneflies 27, Caravel 22.

Caravel scored again — a scrambled ring from the right lodge in the seventeenth minute — but the clock was against them. When the final whistle came, the scoreboard read 27-25, and Dov Marsden was on his knees.

## What It Means

The title is not decided. Not yet. Marsden's sixteen-year wait continues, technically, for one more round. The table after Round 25:

| 1 | Thornbury Lancers | 25 | 51 |
| 2 | Caravel Harriers | 25 | 51 |
| 3 | Ashwick Stoneflies | 25 | 50 |

The Lancers, who beat Coldharbour 32-19 at home on Saturday afternoon — Jens Aldric scoring three rings with his usual quiet efficiency — are level with Caravel at the top. The Stoneflies are one point behind. Three teams, one round remaining, and everything to play for.

Round 26 fixtures, next Saturday: Caravel Harriers host Fernwich Falcons. Thornbury Lancers travel to Dunmore Eagles. Ashwick Stoneflies host Millhaven.

The permutations are simple enough, though they will be argued over in every public house and tram car this week: a Caravel win and a Lancers loss would give the Harriers a third consecutive title. A Lancers win and a Caravel draw or loss gives the title to Thornbury. And if the Stoneflies win and both Caravel and Lancers drop points, Dov Marsden will lift a trophy he has been chasing since he was twenty.

It is unlikely. But then, so was today.

## The Scene

Coach Regan Hollister stood alone at the touchline for a long moment after the whistle, watching his players celebrate, before walking across to shake Sørensen's hand. Sørensen said something that made Hollister nod.

Barlow, whose twelfth ring of the season may prove to be the most important, was interviewed on the pitch by a radio correspondent. He said four words: "We haven't won anything."

Marsden, eventually helped to his feet by Venn and Thatch, said nothing to the press. He walked to the southern end, where the Caravel supporters were still standing, and raised a hand. Then he walked to the tunnel, and he was gone.

In the streets around Ashwick Oval, the crowd spilled out into the Saturday afternoon and did not seem to know where to go. Some walked toward the river. Some stood in groups and talked. A woman outside the Ashwick Arms was crying. A man with a Stoneflies scarf was standing on a bench singing something — a song about sixteen years, if the melody was any guide.

Next Saturday. One more round. Everything still to play for.
