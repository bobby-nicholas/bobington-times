Numbers, as a rule, do not celebrate. They simply move, and leave the interpretation to others.

On Friday, the Eastern Spice Index closed at 298 — its first finish below 300 since the 6th of February, nine days before the Delvarian naval exercises that began this crisis. At its peak, on the 26th of February, the index stood at 356. It has now declined for eleven consecutive sessions. The spice crisis, which emptied shelves and shuttered merchants and forced Simeon Kade to remove his braised lamb shoulder from the menu of the Willow Table for the first time in twelve years, is over — not in principle, but in arithmetic.

Copper closed at 805 florins per tonne, the thirteenth consecutive decline and the lowest since the 4th of February. At 891 — its crisis peak — the metal had implied a tramway copper cost overrun in excess of 600 million florins. At 805, the overrun is approximately 380 million, well within the phased approach parameters endorsed by the Copper Review Commission.

The bond market, which rallied sharply on the 9th of March when council voted unanimously for the phased tramway, has held steady. Twenty-year municipal yields remain at 3.9 per cent — forty basis points below their pre-vote level.

## The Rating Agency

The Continental Rating Agency, which monitors municipal creditworthiness across the commercial nations, has dispatched a senior analyst to Bobington ahead of the tramway bond prospectus. Agnes Whitmore, a principal credit analyst with eighteen years' experience in municipal infrastructure assessment, arrived on Friday and is expected to spend the coming week reviewing the city's fiscal position, the phased approach methodology, and the geological survey programme.

Deputy Treasurer Annabel Whitford confirmed the visit. "This is standard procedure for any municipal bond issuance of this scale," she said. "We welcome the review."

The Rating Agency's current assessment of Bobington's municipal credit is understood to be stable. A downgrade — which would raise borrowing costs by an estimated 40 to 60 basis points — was considered a significant risk when copper stood above 880 and the tramway commitment was unphased. At current prices and with the phased approach in place, that risk has diminished substantially.

The bond prospectus, which will detail the terms and structure of the Phase 1 financing, is on track for circulation by the 31st of March.

## The Stellara

The cargo vessel *Stellara*, carrying a bulk consignment of eastern goods including a substantial velveroot shipment, was sighted off Cape of Sarenne on Friday morning and is expected in Bobington harbour by Monday. If the cargo matches expectations, combined city spice reserves will exceed 1,200 pounds — sufficient through mid-May and, with the strait expected to reopen within weeks, effectively the end of supply anxiety.

The emergency pricing ordinance, referred to the Commerce Committee and never scheduled for debate, is widely expected to be quietly shelved.

## Insurance

The one market that has not moved is insurance. No Bobington underwriter has written a Kaelmar-route policy since the 12th of February. The Transit Corridor Framework's provisions — premiums capped at 140 per cent of pre-crisis, claims via a Fenmouth panel — have been welcomed in principle but not yet in practice.

The insurance market will be the last piece. It will move when the first vessel transits the strait under the new framework — an event expected two to three weeks after the signing. Until then, the Sarenne route remains the only insurable option, and the 14 vessels currently using it will continue to add twelve to fifteen days and considerable cost to every eastern voyage.

The crisis, in sum, is ending. The arithmetic says so. The underwriters, who answer to a different arithmetic, are waiting for the ink.
