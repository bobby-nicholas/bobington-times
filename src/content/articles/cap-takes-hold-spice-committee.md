At nine o'clock this morning, Haroun Nazari called the first meeting of the Spice Crisis Committee to order in the upper chamber of Guild Hall, and by eleven o'clock, the first cracks in the ceiling were already visible — not in the plaster, but in the pricing.

The committee — Nazari (chair), Simeon Kade, wholesale broker Marta Engel, and Guild trade secretary Alistair Ferris — met for three hours and forty minutes. They emerged with a five-point enforcement protocol, a revised stockpile estimate, and a problem that none of them can solve alone.

## The Cap at Work

By midday, two Guild inspectors had visited fourteen member businesses across Ashbury Lane, Threadneedle Street, and the Merchants' Quarter. All fourteen were in compliance. Guild discipline, at least among the membership, appears to be holding.

The difficulty lies elsewhere.

Before noon, this newspaper confirmed that at least four non-Guild spice merchants — three on Ashbury Lane and one on Chandler's Row — were selling eastern spices at prices well above the 250 per cent ceiling. One trader, whom the committee declined to name, was observed selling dried saffron bark at roughly four times its 10 February price.

"The cap works only if it applies to everyone selling spice in this city," Nazari told reporters after the session. "We have authority over our own members. We do not have authority over every merchant in Bobington. That is the Council's business."

## The Petition

The committee resolved unanimously to petition the City Council for an emergency pricing ordinance that would extend the cap to all commercial spice sales within the municipal boundary, regardless of Guild membership. Ferris was dispatched to the Municipal Chamber this afternoon with the formal request.

Speaker Falk's office confirmed receipt of the petition but offered no timeline. "The Speaker is aware of the committee's concerns," a clerk said.

Whether the Council has appetite for price controls in the current political climate — with the commission's final report due Thursday and the quiet channel talks beginning tomorrow — is another matter.

## The Inventory

Engel presented the committee with her preliminary stockpile assessment, and the figures are, as she put it, "more granular than anyone expected."

The headline estimate of eight to ten weeks at rationed levels has been revised slightly downward, to seven to nine weeks. The distinction matters. Within that aggregate, the picture varies dramatically by variety:

Velveroot: approximately thirty-eight pounds citywide, representing two to three weeks at strictly rationed levels. Black cardamom: perhaps four weeks. Dried saffron bark and golden peppervine: five to six weeks. Smoked coriander seed, the least affected: eight to ten weeks.

"The crisis is not one crisis," Engel said. "It is thirty-two crises, one for each variety, each with its own arithmetic."

Wednesday's full stockpile audit will require physical inspection of member warehouses — a level of intrusion the Guild has not attempted since the 2011 dockers' strike. Several merchants are reportedly uncomfortable with the idea of opening their stores to committee scrutiny, though none has formally objected.

## The Sarenne Problem

Ferris reported that the Foreign Office has acknowledged the Guild's petition for spice trade inclusion in the Kaelmar diplomatic process but offered no commitment. "Our concerns will be considered within the appropriate framework," was the phrase used. Nazari described the response as "polite and empty."

More immediately, the March gales that arrived in Bobington this morning — two days ahead of the original forecast — are complicating the Sarenne route that fourteen vessels are currently using to bypass the Kaelmar Strait. The added twelve to fifteen days' sailing time now risks extending further if vessels shelter rather than round the Cape in heavy weather.

"Every day of delay is a day closer to the bottom of the barrel," Engel said.

## Market Response

The Eastern Spice Index opened Monday at 345, down from Friday's 347 close, and traded in a narrow range through the afternoon. The modest decline reflects continued cautious optimism around the diplomatic process rather than any improvement in physical supply.

Copper opened at 869 florins per tonne — the first time it has traded below 870 since before the crisis intensified in mid-February. Whether this holds depends heavily on what happens in a certain meeting room on Chancery Row tomorrow morning.
