At low tide on Saturday morning, with the ferry *Thornhill Star* throttling down to minimum speed as it passed Bramblegate Steps — a precaution requested by the Harbour Authority — Dr Annalise Fenn-Coulthard climbed down the stone embankment with a pair of field glasses, a notebook, and a resolve to count very carefully.

Seven. That is the confirmed number. Five adults and two juveniles, the latter small enough to have been born this winter. They were arranged on the exposed mudflats in the characteristic pattern of a settled colony — adults spaced at intervals, juveniles close to what appeared to be two females, the group oriented toward the water with clear sight lines downriver.

"They are not visiting," Dr Fenn-Coulthard said, brushing river mud from her boots. "Transient seals haul out randomly, rest, and leave. This group has spatial structure. The juveniles are being raised here. This is a colony."

It is the first recorded harbour seal colony in the lower Ashwater since the naturalist Clement Birch documented "a company of harbour seals, eight or ten in number" on these same mudflats in 1891. Industrial discharge — principally from the tanneries and dye works that lined the Docklands waterfront through the early twentieth century — drove the seals downriver and eventually out to sea. The last confirmed sighting in the lower river before this month was in 1923.

## The Conduit Connection

What has brought them back? Dr Fenn-Coulthard believes the answer may lie beneath the wharf.

The Lower Conduit — the buried waterway discovered earlier this year by municipal surveyor Pella Strand — discharges through a stone-arched outfall beneath the old commercial wharf at Bramblegate Steps. The outfall, partially silted but still flowing, introduces a steady current of cooler, fresher water into the tidal mudflat zone.

"The outfall creates a micro-habitat," Dr Fenn-Coulthard explained. "The water temperature at the discharge point is approximately two degrees cooler than the surrounding river, and the salinity is lower. Harbour seals prefer exactly this kind of interface — brackish, sheltered, with good haul-out access. The conduit is, in effect, making the mudflat habitable again."

The improvement in water quality over the past two decades — the result of the closure of the remaining riverside industrial works and the upgrade of the municipal drainage system — has also contributed. The river is cleaner than it has been since before the First Industrial Survey of 1898.

Reg Garside, the retired harbour pilot who first spotted the colony from his bench on Harbourfront Parade on the 11th of March, was present on Saturday morning. He sat on the same bench, watching the survey through a pair of naval binoculars that he claims are older than he is.

"Best thing I've seen on this river in fifteen years," Garside said, as he has said before, and will presumably continue to say until someone finds something better.

## Expert Assistance

Dr Fenn-Coulthard has contacted Dr Lucian Ware, a marine mammal specialist at Caldwell University and the author of *Pinnipeds of the Western Seaboard*, the standard reference work on harbour seal populations in the region. Dr Ware has agreed to visit Bobington next week to assist with the population assessment and advise on habitat protection measures.

"A breeding colony establishing itself in an urban river after more than a century is genuinely significant," Dr Ware said by telephone on Friday. "If the habitat conditions are sustained, this colony could grow. But it will need protection from disturbance — boat traffic, construction noise, well-meaning crowds."

The Harbour Authority confirmed on Saturday that the request for a temporary wildlife protection zone covering the mudflats below Bramblegate Steps is "under active consideration" and that a decision is expected before the end of the month. In the meantime, the Authority has asked ferry operators to reduce speed when passing the colony at low tide — a request to which Captain Aldgate of the *Thornhill Star* has readily complied.

"I have been on this river for twenty-six years," Aldgate said. "I can spare thirty seconds."

The seals, for their part, appeared entirely indifferent to the survey, the ferry, and the retired harbour pilot watching them from his bench. At high tide, they slipped into the water and vanished. At low tide, they will return.

They have, after all, been doing this for rather longer than we have.
