She brought the flowers in a canvas bag. Sixty-three chrysanthemums, white, bought the day before from Mrs Gowan's stall at Bramblegate Market. Michael carried the bag from the motorcar to the mine entrance, a distance of perhaps two hundred yards along a path that used to be a rail track, and set it down on the concrete apron in front of the sealed shaft.

Bess Holloway, eighty-one years old, bent slowly and began to lay them out. One at a time. She has done this every year since 1964, the first anniversary. In the early years, she brought forty-one — one for each man killed in the collapse of the No. 3 shaft on 14 March 1963. At some point — she does not remember when, or perhaps does not wish to say — she began to bring one for each year instead.

"It seemed right," she said afterwards. "The men don't need counting. Everyone knows how many there were. But the years — the years need counting. Someone has to count them."

## The Ceremony

Father Dennis Holt, the parish priest of St. Edmund's in Dunvale, led a brief service at ten o'clock. There were fourteen people present — fewer than in recent years, Bess noted, because "the ones who remember are getting fewer." Michael stood beside his mother with his cap in his hands. Two women from the Dunvale Historical Society read the names of the forty-one. A wreath from the Miners' Cooperative of Greymoor was placed beside the flowers.

Father Holt, who has led this service for twenty-two years — arriving in the parish in 2004, a full forty-one years after the disaster — spoke briefly about memory and obligation.

"I did not know these men," he said. "But I know this place, and I know what it owes them, and I know that Bess Holloway has been more faithful to their memory than any monument could be."

The sky was overcast. The wind came from the northwest, carrying the smell of wet heather. The sealed mine entrance — concrete and iron, stamped with the date of closure, 1964 — was damp.

## The Memorial

For the first time in sixty-three years, there is something else on the hillside.

Thirty metres above the mine entrance, on a cleared and levelled platform of Greymoor granite, the foundation work for the national memorial is visible. Steel reinforcing rods protrude from the concrete in a rough rectangle. The groundwork was completed last week by a crew from Hallam & Stroud. The memorial — forty-one bronze figures ascending a staircase, designed by sculptor Ines Cavallo of Port Caravel — is expected to be installed in stages through the spring and autumn.

Bess looked up at the foundation on Friday morning and did not say anything for a while.

"I thought I would die before they started," she said eventually. "I very nearly did. I started asking in 1968. They kept saying it wasn't the right time. They kept saying that."

Michael, who was two years old when his father was killed, who grew up in a house shaped by the disaster and went into the mines himself at seventeen — thirty-one years at the Edgeminster colliery before retirement — drove his mother back to Edgeminster afterwards. They stopped in Dunvale village for tea. The café owner, who knows them, did not charge.

"I was too young to remember him," Michael said, as he has said before, and as he will likely say again. "I remember her remembering him. That was enough. That was everything."

The chrysanthemums will remain at the mine entrance until the wind takes them. No one removes them. No one ever has.
